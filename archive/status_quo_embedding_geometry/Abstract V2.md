## Abstract 
Large language models (LLMs) have been shown to exhibit surface-level political orientations, but less attention has been paid to whether their representational structure encodes a more fundamental asymmetry: the systematic treatment of social change as ontologically deviant and existing social order as normal. Drawing on Althusser's concept of Ideological State Apparatuses, Marcuse's analysis of one-dimensional thought, and Foucault's theory of normalization, we argue that LLM embedding geometry constitutes a measurable site of ideological reproduction — one that operates prior to and independently of explicit political content. Using the Word Embedding Association Test (WEAT) across two axes — normalcy/deviance (primary) and stability/disruption (secondary) — and masked token completion tasks, we test whether concepts associated with social change are geometrically closer to deviance-adjacent semantic fields than concepts associated with social order. Preliminary results on BERT-base show consistent negative effect sizes on both axes (d = −0.48, d = −0.32), with _market_ emerging as the most "natural" concept in the dataset and _redistribution_ as the least. A cross-model comparison reveals that multilingual BERT strengthens the normalcy/deviance asymmetry (d = −0.66) while reversing the disruption axis (+0.43), suggesting the two dimensions of status quo bias are not equivalent across linguistic corpora. We conduct a comparative audit across models representing distinct training origins, including models developed with explicit regional sovereignty mandates (LatamGPT, Sea-Lion), and discuss implications for AI as a cultural apparatus shaping the imaginability of social change.
