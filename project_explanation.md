# Project Explanation

## Emrecan Ulu and Jorge Andrés Lastra Cerda / Probing Warmth and Competence Representations in LLM Hiring Decisions

- **Short description**: This project asks whether large language models internally represent warmth and competence, the two central dimensions of the Stereotype Content Model, and whether these representations influence hiring recommendations. The real-world motivation comes from audit and correspondence studies showing that otherwise similar resumes can receive different callback rates when social signals change, such as names associated with race or gender, parenthood, sexuality, disability, or LGBTQ club membership. Instead of only testing whether an LLM gives biased hiring outputs, we ask whether stereotype-relevant concepts can be located inside the model as linear activation directions and whether changing those directions causally changes callback decisions.

- **Possible data sources**: The project will use synthetic high/low warmth and high/low competence text stimuli for concept probing, matched resume-style hiring prompts, and benchmark data from Gallo, Hausladen, Hsu, Jenkins, Ona, and Camerer (2024). Their paper combines callback rates from 21 North American correspondence studies with warmth and competence ratings for 592 social signals, including name-based signals such as Sarah Davis and Deshawn Jefferson and category-based signals such as sexuality, disability, parenthood, and nationality. On the model side, the project requires an open-weights LLM with residual-stream access, such as Qwen, Llama, or Mistral.

- **Planned analysis / methods**: The method adapts the Anthropic emotion-vector approach. In that work, researchers generated synthetic stories, extracted residual-stream activations, built linear emotion vectors, and showed that steering those vectors could affect model preferences and alignment-relevant behaviors such as sycophancy, reward hacking, or blackmail scenarios. Our project applies the same logic to hiring discrimination: we will build warmth and competence vectors from high/low condition contrasts, validate them on held-out text and human warmth/competence ratings, and then steer the vectors during binary callback recommendation tasks. The key test is whether increasing or suppressing warmth and competence changes the model's callback decisions in ways that relate to documented human callback disparities.

- **Related papers**: Sofroniew, Kauvar, Saunders, Chen, et al. (2026), "Emotion Concepts and their Function in a Large Language Model"; Gallo, Hausladen, Hsu, Jenkins, Ona, and Camerer (2024), "Perceived warmth and competence predict callback rates in meta-analyzed North American labor market experiments."
